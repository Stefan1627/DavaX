# 1. Executive Summary

This business case recommends proceeding with the development of an AI-enabled **Contract Review Assistant** for legal, procurement, sales operations, and contract management teams. The proposed solution is a high-feasibility use case that applies large language models and contract-specific rules to accelerate review of contracts, identify risk, detect deviations from approved language, extract obligations and key dates, and support more consistent decision-making.

The primary business problem is that manual contract review is often slow, inconsistent, and heavily dependent on a limited number of legal or commercial subject-matter experts. As contract volumes grow, organizations face increasing turnaround time, review bottlenecks, compliance exposure, and opportunity cost from delayed approvals and sales cycles.

The Contract Review Assistant does not replace legal professionals. It acts as a decision-support tool that performs first-pass analysis, highlights high-risk issues, and routes sensitive or non-standard matters to qualified reviewers. This human-in-the-loop design makes the use case practical, governable, and aligned with enterprise risk management expectations.

From a business perspective, the opportunity is compelling. The solution can reduce contract review effort, improve review consistency, surface obligations and renewal risks earlier, and enable legal teams to focus on higher-value advisory work. From a technical perspective, feasibility is high because contracts are document-based, review criteria are often codified in templates and playbooks, and modern NLP/LLM capabilities are well suited to extraction, comparison, summarization, and risk flagging tasks.

Based on reasonable assumptions, organizations with moderate-to-high contract volume can expect meaningful productivity gains, reduced legal backlog, faster contract cycle times, and better compliance visibility. The recommendation is to proceed with a phased implementation beginning with a pilot on selected contract types and well-defined review rules.

# 2. Business Problem

Manual contract review remains a time-intensive and resource-constrained process in many organizations. Legal teams, procurement specialists, sales operations staff, and contract managers are often required to review large numbers of agreements under tight timelines, while balancing risk, commercial priorities, and compliance obligations.

Several persistent inefficiencies make this process costly and difficult to scale:

**Turnaround time is slow.** Reviewers must manually read lengthy documents, compare language to approved templates, identify deviations, and determine whether unusual clauses require escalation. This increases review cycle time, especially during procurement events, quarter-end sales periods, renewals, or peak operational periods.

**Bottlenecks form around legal specialists.** Contracts that contain non-standard language or potential risk are often routed to a small number of experienced legal reviewers. This creates dependency on scarce expertise and leads to backlogs, delayed approvals, and reduced responsiveness to the business.

**Human error risk is material.** Manual review can miss subtle but important issues, including missing clauses, unfavorable liability terms, weak data protection provisions, non-standard indemnities, auto-renewal language, termination restrictions, or untracked obligations. Under time pressure, these risks increase.

**Review quality is inconsistent.** Different reviewers may interpret policies, fallback language, and risk thresholds differently. This leads to variability in outcomes, uneven application of standards, and inconsistent contracting positions across teams or regions.

**Compliance exposure increases.** Organizations are under greater pressure to comply with privacy, regulatory, procurement, and audit requirements. Inadequate review processes can lead to missed compliance issues, unmonitored obligations, and insufficient documentation of why a contract was approved.

In effect, the current process is often too manual for the volume, pace, and governance expectations faced by modern organizations. This creates both operational inefficiency and business risk.

# 3. Proposed Solution

The proposed solution is an AI-powered **Contract Review Assistant** that supports first-pass contract analysis and augments human reviewers with structured insights, risk alerts, and workflow support.

The system would review uploaded or ingested contract documents and produce a guided analysis based on company-approved templates, clause libraries, review playbooks, and business rules. It would focus on identifying issues that are repetitive, document-heavy, and suitable for assisted review.

Core functions include:

**Clause extraction.** The system identifies and extracts key clauses and metadata such as governing law, limitation of liability, indemnity, payment terms, confidentiality, data protection terms, termination rights, renewal conditions, service levels, and dispute resolution language.

**Risk flagging.** It highlights potentially risky provisions based on predefined policy rules, approved fallback language, and risk thresholds. Examples include uncapped liability, one-sided indemnities, excessive penalties, missing data privacy clauses, or unfavorable termination conditions.

**Deviation analysis.** It compares the contract against company-approved templates or standard fallback language and identifies where language is missing, altered, or materially non-standard.

**Obligation tracking.** It extracts operational obligations, notice periods, milestones, service commitments, reporting requirements, renewal dates, and termination windows to improve downstream monitoring.

**Summary generation.** It produces concise summaries for reviewers, including key terms, notable deviations, high-risk items, missing protections, and recommended escalation points.

**Workflow integration.** It connects with contract lifecycle management systems, document repositories, ticketing systems, approval workflows, and collaboration tools to support review routing, escalation, and auditability.

The intended operating model is not autonomous legal judgment. Rather, the assistant performs structured analysis, proposes findings, and enables human reviewers to validate, reject, or escalate issues. High-risk or ambiguous matters remain under legal control.

# 4. Target Users and Stakeholders

**Legal teams.** In-house counsel, legal operations, and contract attorneys are primary users. They benefit from reduced first-pass review burden, improved issue triage, and better allocation of legal attention to high-risk matters.

**Procurement.** Procurement and sourcing teams often manage supplier agreements, purchase terms, and service contracts. They benefit from quicker identification of non-standard supplier terms, missing protections, and renewal-related obligations.

**Sales and commercial teams.** Sales operations and commercial managers need faster turnaround on customer contracts to avoid delays in deal closure. They benefit from quicker redlining support, deviation analysis, and earlier escalation of problematic clauses.

**Compliance and risk teams.** These stakeholders benefit from improved visibility into policy adherence, compliance-related clauses, and auditable review practices across the contract portfolio.

**Senior management.** Executives in legal, finance, operations, procurement, and commercial leadership benefit from faster cycle times, better productivity, reduced legal exposure, and stronger operational control over contracting.

# 5. Value Proposition

The Contract Review Assistant offers value across operational, risk, and strategic dimensions.

**Faster contract review.** By automating first-pass analysis and pre-identifying issues, the solution reduces the time required to review standard and semi-standard agreements.

**Reduced legal risk.** The system improves the likelihood that high-risk clauses, missing protections, and non-compliant language are detected and escalated appropriately.

**Greater standardization.** By applying approved templates, clause libraries, and review rules consistently, the solution reduces reviewer variability and supports a more uniform contracting posture.

**Better visibility into obligations and renewals.** Extracting obligations, deadlines, and renewal dates helps prevent missed notices, unmanaged commitments, and avoidable commercial leakage.

**Lower operational cost.** The assistant can reduce manual effort, improve legal team productivity, and decrease the cost of processing recurring contract reviews.

The value proposition is strongest in organizations where contract volume is moderate to high, template adherence matters, and turnaround time has direct business impact.

# 6. Market / Organizational Need

Organizations increasingly need this capability for several reasons.

First, **contract volumes are rising**. Growth in supplier ecosystems, software procurement, outsourcing, partnerships, and commercial transactions has expanded the number and variety of contracts that require review.

Second, there is greater **pressure for faster deal cycles**. Sales teams need legal review to keep pace with commercial deadlines, while procurement teams need timely contract completion to support operations and sourcing events. Slow review has direct revenue and operational consequences.

Third, organizations face increased **compliance and audit expectations**. Privacy requirements, sector-specific obligations, internal controls, and governance demands require more structured and defensible contract review processes.

Fourth, many organizations are under pressure to do more with constrained headcount. Legal and contract management teams are expected to support business growth without proportionate increases in staffing. AI-assisted review is therefore attractive as a scalable productivity lever.

Finally, enterprise adoption of document intelligence, workflow platforms, and AI copilots has created a more favorable environment for practical deployment. Contract review is now a priority area because it combines measurable inefficiency with a clear governance need.

# 7. Feasibility Assessment

**Feasibility rating: High**

This use case is highly feasible for both technical and operational reasons.

**Data availability is generally strong.** Most organizations already store contracts, templates, fallback clauses, redline playbooks, approval histories, and review notes in shared drives, CLM platforms, or document management systems. These materials provide the foundation for prompt design, retrieval, rules configuration, and evaluation.

**The task aligns well with mature NLP and LLM capabilities.** Modern language models are well suited to document parsing, clause extraction, summarization, issue identification, and comparison against standard language. These are established enterprise use cases with growing implementation maturity.

**Enterprise workflows are familiar and structured.** Contract review typically follows defined steps: intake, draft review, issue identification, escalation, redlining, approval, and execution. This makes it easier to embed AI into existing workflows rather than forcing a new operating model.

**Human-in-the-loop review is practical.** Unlike fully autonomous decision systems, this solution can be constrained to supporting human review. That reduces governance risk and allows the organization to set clear confidence thresholds, escalation rules, and approval responsibilities.

**Scope can be controlled.** The implementation can begin with a narrow set of contract types, such as NDAs, MSAs, supplier agreements, or standard sales contracts, and expand gradually. This makes quality assurance and adoption more manageable.

**The value is measurable.** Review time, backlog, escalation rate, clause detection accuracy, and cycle time can all be tracked. This supports a disciplined pilot and ROI evaluation.

Feasibility remains high provided the organization has usable templates and review guidance, executive sponsorship, and appropriate controls for confidentiality and validation.

# 9. Risks and Limitations

Despite the strong feasibility, the solution has important risks and limitations.

**Hallucinations or inaccurate interpretation.** AI may misread legal nuance, incorrectly classify clause risk, or infer conclusions not supported by the contract text. Legal language can be context-dependent, and subtle wording differences can materially change meaning.

**Confidentiality and data privacy concerns.** Contracts frequently contain commercially sensitive data, personal data, pricing details, and regulated information. Improper data handling or insecure deployment would create unacceptable risk.

**Over-reliance on AI.** Users may place undue trust in AI-generated summaries or risk flags and fail to conduct appropriate human review, especially if the tool appears confident.

**Integration complexity.** Connecting the assistant to CLM platforms, document repositories, approval workflows, and identity controls may be more complex than the initial proof of concept suggests.

**Need for legal validation.** Risk rules, clause libraries, fallback language, and escalation thresholds must be defined and maintained by qualified legal stakeholders. Without this, outputs may be inconsistent or not aligned to policy.

**Change management risk.** Adoption may be limited if users do not trust the outputs, if the workflow adds friction, or if the solution is positioned unrealistically as a replacement for judgment rather than a support tool.

# 10. Risk Mitigation

The recommended risk mitigation approach is practical and governance-oriented.

**Human-in-the-loop review.** AI outputs should be advisory. Final approval, legal interpretation, and exception handling remain with authorized human reviewers, especially for high-risk or non-standard contracts.

**Restricted scope.** Begin with specific contract types, known templates, and well-defined review criteria. Avoid broad deployment across highly bespoke agreements until performance is proven.

**Approved playbooks and templates.** Use legal-approved clause libraries, fallback positions, risk rules, and escalation logic to constrain outputs and align the system with organizational policy.

**Audit trails.** Record the contract version reviewed, issues flagged, actions taken, reviewer decisions, and model outputs. This supports defensibility, governance, and continuous improvement.

**Access controls and secure deployment.** Enforce role-based access, encryption, logging, and secure hosting. Sensitive contract data should be handled within enterprise-grade security boundaries.

**Validation and testing.** Evaluate the assistant against historical contracts and legal review outcomes before production release. Accuracy thresholds should be established for clause extraction, deviation detection, and risk flagging.

**User training.** Users should be trained on intended use, limitations, confidence levels, and when escalation is mandatory.

# 11. Implementation Approach

A phased implementation is recommended.

## Phase 1: Pilot with limited contract types

Start with one or two high-volume, lower-variance contract categories, such as NDAs, supplier agreements, or standard customer contracts. Configure approved templates, risk rules, and escalation criteria. Measure review time, issue-detection performance, and user feedback.

**Pilot objectives**

* Validate core use cases
* Establish baseline and post-pilot metrics
* Test human-in-the-loop workflow
* Identify false positives and false negatives
* Refine prompts, rules, and reporting

## Phase 2: Integration with CLM and document systems

Integrate the assistant into existing contract intake and review workflows. Connect to CLM platforms, document repositories, identity systems, ticketing tools, or approval systems as needed. Introduce obligation extraction, renewal tracking, and standardized summaries.

**Phase 2 objectives**

* Improve workflow fit
* Reduce manual copying and re-entry
* Strengthen auditability and adoption
* Expand reporting and operational visibility

## Phase 3: Scale to more departments and contract categories

Extend the capability to additional departments, geographies, and contract families, including more complex agreements where appropriate. Introduce department-specific policies and broader analytics.

**Phase 3 objectives**

* Increase value capture across the contract lifecycle
* Support enterprise standardization
* Improve portfolio-wide visibility into risk, obligations, and cycle time

**Reduction in legal review backlog.** Number of pending contracts awaiting legal review compared with baseline.

Additional useful metrics include:

* False positive and false negative rates for risk flags
* Percentage of contracts requiring escalation

A realistic KPI strategy should prioritize measurable operational improvement over unrealistic claims of near-perfect legal accuracy.
